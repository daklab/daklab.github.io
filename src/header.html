Knowles lab

New York Genome Center / Columbia University

We develop and apply machine learning, statistical genetics, and functional genomics methods to dissect the causal chain from genetic variation to trait, especially human disease. We're particularly interested in RNA splicing changes as a molecular mediator of genetic risk. We collaborate with diverse research groups at NYGC and beyond collecting large-scale genomics datasets in the context of neurodegenerative and neuropsychiatric disease, and developing novel genomic technologies including single cell methods, forward genetic screens and long-read transcriptomics.

If you're interested in machine-learning-flavored genomics, statisical genetics, functional genomics and/or RNA splicing, please get in touch! Postdocs and students are eligible for an appointment at Columbia University.

David teaches COMS4762, a computer science class at Columbia on machine learning in genetics (Columbia LionMail account required for access), usually in the fall. He also co-chairs the annual Machine Learning in Computational Biology meeting (Youtube channel!).

Research overview

Quantifying alternative splicing across genotypes, cell-types and disease states. Dr Knowles previously developed LeafCutter, a scalable, flexible approach to quantifying alternative splicing (AS) from bulk RNA-seq. LeafCutter represents AS via the usage rate of each splice junction relative to a group of neighboring junctions. We and others have used LeafCutter to show that for typical complex diseases a comparable number of risk variants act through AS vs expression. We are working on tools for single-cell, long-read and spatial AS analysis, as well as ways to interpret the remarkable isoform complexity of human cells revealed by long-read RNA-seq.

QTL mapping in thousands of brain samples. RNA-seq sample sizes for brain have lagged behind other tissues. With the Raj lab we have uniformly processed over 10,000 post-mortem bulk brain RNA-seq samples, a resource we call BigBrain. BigBrain is the largest brain quantitative trait locus (QTL) meta-analysis to date so the resulting QTLs are providing unprecedented resolution to dissect neurological disease GWAS loci. In a parallel effort, SingleBrain, we meta-analysed brain single nuclei RNA-seq from ~1000 individuals to map cell-type & state specific expression QTLs.

Deep learning models of gene regulation for variant effect prediction. QTL mapping requires large sample sizes and is only practical for common genetic variants and cell-types/states. By contrast, sequence-to-function deep learning (DL) models trained to predict chromatin marks, expression or splicing, from DNA sequence alone, only need data from a few individuals. Dr Knowles was involved in developing SpliceAI, one of the earlier methods using modern DL to do this, specifically for splice sites. The predicted difference between the reference and alternate alleles (delta scores) can be powerful predictors of variant effects, even for previously unseen variants. As part of the Alzheimer's Disease Sequencing Project (ADSP) we are training and assessing DL models of chromatin accessibility, histone modifications and splicing in AD relevant cell types. Delta scores for microglial chromatin accessibility are strongly enriched in AD GWAS and enable improved functional fine-mapping. We developed new rare variant association models to leverage these delta scores and find new genetic associations with AD.

Causal gene regulatory network inference. DL models and cis QTL mapping help link molecular changes to disease but don't model how these effects propagate through the gene regulatory network (GRN). Building on our earlier work on robust, scalable Mendelian randomization, we developed inverse sparse regression, which deconvolves an estimated total causal effect network into direct and indirect effects using a directed analogue of the graphical lasso. We are applying inspre to trans-eQTLs from BigBrain and neurological GWAS to map how genetic effects propagate through the GRN to impact disease risk. To adapt these ideas to the nonlinear, multiple cell-type setting we have developed and theoretically characterized differential equation models of cellular dynamics.

Experimental characterization of isoform function. It is currently challenging to characterise the function of putative disease relevant isoforms. To address this, we developed a strategy targeting unique exon-exon junctions in mature RNA with CRISPR/Cas13d to knockdown specific isoforms and, in collaboration with the Sanjana lab, a DL model, TIGER, to predict guide RNA efficacy for this system.

Foundational machine learning. We also work on foundational ML, most recently on kernel-based latent subspace analysis, principled priors for variational autoencoders, and heteroskedastic regression.

People

David A. Knowles, Principal Investigator

I was a postdoc at Stanford with Sylvia Plevritis (Computational Systems Biology/Radiology) and Jonathan Pritchard (Genetics) having previously worked with Daphne Koller. Apart from working on splicing (see above), I developed EAGLE to detect gene-by-environment (GxE) effects on the transcriptomic using allele-specific expression and a novel, efficient linear mixed model, suez which we used to ls to understand the genetic basis of anthracycline-cardiotoxicity using iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes. I did my PhD with Zoubin Ghahramani in the Machine Learning group of the Cambridge University Engineering Department, where I worked on Bayesian non-parametric models and (stochastic) variational inference. I was the Roger Needham Scholar at Wolfson College, funded by Microsoft Research. My undergraduate degree comprised two years of Physics before switching to Engineering to complete an MEng with Zoubin. I took the MSc Bioinformatics and Systems Biology at Imperial College in 2007/8. CV.
